The First Hero:
Gilgamesh and the Story That Started It All

Mythology Archive • Volume I

Before knights searched for dragons, before fantasy worlds were filled with kingdoms, prophecies and immortal beings, there was Gilgamesh.

Written thousands of years ago in ancient Mesopotamia, The Epic of Gilgamesh remains one of humanity's oldest surviving stories.

Yet despite its age, the questions at its heart remain familiar:

What does it mean to be remembered?
Can power protect us from loss?
How do we accept the limits of our own existence?

A King Between Legend and History

Gilgamesh stands between history and legend.

He was believed to have been a ruler of Uruk, one of the great cities of ancient Mesopotamia. Over generations, his story grew beyond history and became one of humanity's earliest myths.

His strength became legendary. His victories became larger than life. But what allowed his name to survive was not simply his power.

It was his humanity.

The Friendship That Changed the Hero

At the centre of Gilgamesh's journey is not conquest, but friendship.

Enkidu enters his life as an equal — someone who can challenge the king and force him to see beyond himself.

Through Enkidu, Gilgamesh discovers compassion, loyalty and connection.

The hero who once believed himself above humanity begins to understand what it means to belong among it.

The First Search for Immortality

After losing Enkidu, Gilgamesh faces the one enemy no warrior can defeat:

Mortality.

His grief sends him on a journey beyond kingdoms and into the unknown, searching for a way to escape the fate awaiting all humans.

But the lesson he discovers is not that death can be conquered.

It is that a life becomes eternal through memory, through the people we touch, and through the stories that remain after us.

Why Gilgamesh Still Inspires Fantasy

The influence of Gilgamesh can still be found throughout modern storytelling:

  • The powerful ruler discovering humanity.
  • The warrior confronting grief.
  • The search for immortality.
  • The desire to leave behind a legacy.

The worlds may change, but the questions remain.

Ancient myths survive because they were never only stories about gods or kings.

They were always stories about us.

Writer's Note

The Epic of Gilgamesh inspired my own mythological retelling, Threads of Babylonia, exploring Gilgamesh, Aetira, destiny and the fragile relationship between power and humanity.

While my story journeys into fantasy, the heart remains connected to the ancient epic:

The greatest battles are not always fought against enemies. Sometimes they are fought within ourselves.
